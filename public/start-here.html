Newly diagnosed guide

Start here — your first 90 days with T1D

A diagnosis is overwhelming. This guide tells you what to focus on first — and what can wait. You don't need to learn everything at once.

"When our daughter was diagnosed, we felt like we had to master everything immediately. We didn't. You don't either. Diabetes management is a skill built over months and years, not days."

— A T1D parent who has been where you are

Phase 1

🔴 First 7 days — survive and stabilise

1

Follow your care team's instructions exactly

You'll have a diabetes educator, endocrinologist or paediatrician. In the first week, their guidance overrides everything else — including advice from other parents online.

2

Learn to read the CGM — not react to every number

The CGM (Dexcom, Libre) shows glucose every 5 minutes. You'll be tempted to act on every reading. In week one, focus on understanding the trend arrows, not the individual numbers.

3

Know the hypo protocol — this is the most important thing

A hypo (low glucose, typically below 4 mmol/L) needs fast-acting carbohydrates immediately. Know what to use, how much, and when to call for help. Your care team will give you a specific protocol.

4

Rest and be kind to yourself

The emotional weight of a new diagnosis is real. Grief, fear, and exhaustion are all normal. You are not failing — you are learning something genuinely difficult.

Phase 2

🟡 First 30 days — build the foundations

1

Understand why glucose rises and falls

Glucose goes up with food (especially carbohydrates) and comes down with insulin and activity. Start noticing which foods cause the biggest rises, and how quickly.

2

Learn about carb counting — imperfectly

You don't need to be precise immediately. Start with rough estimates and refine over time. A food scale helps, but many families manage without one.

3

Notice your child's patterns — don't just react to readings

Does glucose always rise in the morning? Does sport always cause a low? Start noticing patterns — this is the beginning of true understanding.

4

Find your community

Other T1D families understand in a way that others don't. Facebook groups, local JDRF/Breakthrough T1D networks, and school nurse connections can all be invaluable.

Phase 3

🟢 First 90 days — develop real understanding

1

Understand the "big five" glucose patterns

Delayed meal spikes (pizza, high-fat meals), overnight rises (dawn phenomenon), exercise spikes and lows, sick-day highs, and hormonal patterns. Once you can name them, they become manageable.

2

Build school and activity routines

Sport, excursions, birthday parties, sleepovers — these all require planning. Build a simple protocol for each, with your care team's input.

3

Start explaining patterns, not just recording numbers

Move from "her glucose was 14 after dinner" to "her glucose rose 3 hours after the pizza because of the fat content slowing digestion." This shift changes everything.

4

Help your child understand their own diabetes

Age-appropriate diabetes education for the child themselves builds independence and resilience. They are not broken. They are managing something genuinely complex — and doing it every single day.
